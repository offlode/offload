## 1. Weight-Based Pricing

### Industry Standard Rate Ranges

Weight-based pricing (price per pound) is the dominant model for wash & fold laundry services in the U.S. The standard range varies substantially by market tier:

| Market Tier | Price per Pound | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Rural / Small Town (drop-off) | $0.99 – $1.75 | Minimal overhead, self-service/drop-off only |
| Suburban (drop-off) | $1.00 – $1.75 | Lower rent, community laundromats |
| Urban Center (drop-off) | $1.75 – $2.50 | Standard walk-in or drop-off |
| Urban Center (pickup & delivery) | $2.00 – $3.50+ | Adds driver cost, logistics overhead |
| Premium / NYC-tier (P&D) | $3.49 – $5.00 | White-glove, separate-load guarantee |
| Same-Day / Rush (any tier) | +$0.50–$1.00/lb or +25–50% | Expedited processing premium |

**Key data points:**
- [Columbia Pike Laundry](https://www.columbiapikelaundry.com/post/per-pound-laundry-pricing-guide-en) cites the industry consensus at **$1.75–$3.50/lb** for most residential wash & fold with pickup/delivery.
- [Todd Layne Cleaners (NYC)](https://toddlaynecleaners.com/how-much-should-wash-and-fold-laundry-cost-in-nyc/) charges **$3.49/lb** walk-in and **$3.89/lb** for delivery—and argues anything below $2.95/lb likely involves mixing loads or outsourcing to commercial facilities.
- [Craft Cleaners](https://craftcleaners.com/is-wash-and-fold-laundry-service-worth-it-comparing-costs-vs-doing-it-yourself/) reports the national average at **$1.50–$3.00/lb** with a typical 10–15 lb minimum.

### Standard Weight Calculation Method

The industry standard is to weigh laundry **after it is dry**, not wet. This prevents customers from being charged for water weight absorbed during washing.

**How it works in practice:**
1. Customer drops off or has laundry picked up
2. Laundry is washed, dried, folded, and packed
3. Weight is measured on a postal or postal-style scale **after** drying
4. Customer is billed: `(dry weight in lbs) × (rate per lb)` with any applicable minimums or add-on fees

[Poplin](https://poplin.co/blog/how-much-does-poplin-cost-lets-talk-dollars-and-pounds) explicitly states: *"Your laundry is weighed after it's dry. It's not weighed when it's wet."* They suggest a bathroom-scale trick for verification: weigh yourself, then weigh yourself holding the bag, subtract.

**Typical garment weights for reference:**
- 3 T-shirts ≈ 1 lb
- Jeans ≈ 1.5 lbs
- Hoodie ≈ 2 lbs
- Full hamper ≈ 10 lbs
- Average household weekly load ≈ 20–60 lbs

### Minimum Order Policies

Minimums exist to ensure each order covers the fixed costs of pickup, processing, and delivery regardless of load size.

| Service Type | Typical Minimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Drop-off (in-store) | $15–$38 or 10–12 lbs | Covers sorting/processing labor |
| Pickup & Delivery | $30–$50 | Covers driver cost + processing |
| Poplin (Standard) | $30 | Applied if weight × rate < $30 |
| Poplin (Express) | $40 | Applied if weight × rate < $40 |
| Rinse (Wash & Fold) | $30 | Per bag minimum |
| Columbia Pike | $38 (drop-off), $50 (P&D) | Stated on their pricing page |

Minimums are typically enforced by charging the minimum even if the actual load falls below it. Some services (like Poplin) allow small loads but guarantee the floor charge.

### How Platforms Structure Customizations

**Rinse:** Customers select detergent type (scented vs. hypoallergenic) in account preferences. No explicit per-order upcharge — included in the per-lb rate. Cleaning preferences set once and applied to all orders.

**Poplin:** Customers select at order time from: (1) Classic Scented, (2) Unscented Hypoallergenic, (3) "I Will Provide." Fabric softener not generally used but can be provided by customer at no extra charge. "Additional instructions" field allows any special request.

**Hampr:** Customers set preferences in the app. Washrs are expected to use specific products (this is a pain point — some Washrs complain about required detergent cost).

**Traditional Laundromats:** Most use a per-load flat fee for add-ons (bleach, fabric softener, specialty detergent) or a small per-pound surcharge.

---

### Upsell Strategy: How Platforms Extract More Revenue from Customizations

**1. Tiered base pricing anchored on convenience, not add-ons**  
Platforms like Poplin use a simple $1/lb rate but double it for express ($2/lb). This is the primary upsell — urgency is the clearest value driver. Customers who upgrade to express spend 2× the base amount on the exact same load.

**2. Subscription lock-in with bundled perks**  
Rinse's Repeat subscription converts volatile PAYG customers ($3.29/lb + fees) into predictable recurring revenue at $1.64/lb. The subscription bundles: waived delivery fee ($9.95), waived service fee ($5.00), waived rush fee ($9.95), + $10/mo credit. A customer who does 2 orders/month saves ~$30 in fees alone — but the subscription may lock in 4 bags/month of volume. This is classic SaaS-style retention applied to laundry.

**3. Flat add-ons as easy "yes" decisions**  
Items like "add OxiClean ($0.75)" or "hang dry ($0.75/item)" are priced low enough that most customers click without thinking — very high attach rate, very high margin relative to cost.

**4. Minimum order floors drive basket size**  
A $30 minimum means a customer with 8 lbs of laundry at $1/lb still pays $30. This functions as an implicit upcharge on small orders and encourages customers to "fill the bag" — increasing average order weight.

**5. Bulky item surcharges**  
Oversized items (comforters, duvets) are priced separately at a fixed premium ($8–$40/item) because they require a dedicated machine cycle. This captures true incremental cost and adds $8–$40 of revenue per item without touching the base rate.

**6. Rush as the highest-margin add-on**  
Doubling the per-lb price for express (or charging $9.95–$15 flat) has minimal additional cost — the laundry is done at the same facility. The premium reflects time reallocation, not material cost.

### Optimal Take Rate for a New Platform vs. Established

**Framework (from [TechCrunch marketplace analysis](https://techcrunch.com/2021/11/17/4-strategies-for-setting-marketplace-take-rates/)):**

> *"Maximizing take rate is not the goal. A higher take rate leads to lower transaction volume."*

Key principles for setting take rate:

| Stage | Recommended Take Rate | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Launch / Pre-scale** | **10–15%** | Reduce friction; attract supply-side (laundromats/washers); grow GMV |
| **Growth** | **15–20%** | As liquidity improves, capture more value; competitors have ~25–30% |
| **Scale / Dominant** | **20–30%** | At network effect moat, match peers; add value-added services to justify |

**Factors that support a higher take rate in laundry:**
- Fragmented supply (thousands of laundromats vs. a few airlines) — higher take rate justified
- High transaction frequency (weekly laundry) — healthy LTV supports investment in low take rate early
- Platform drives truly incremental demand for laundromats (orders they wouldn't have otherwise gotten)
- Quality verification and trust (background checks, insurance) — justifies 2–5% premium over unmanaged marketplace

**Factors that push take rate down:**
- Physical service with real material costs — workers/laundromats have thin margins
- Worker churn is a major risk — if take is too high, best Washers leave for direct customers
- New entrant competition — fighting for supply means being worker-friendly

**Practical recommendation for a new platform:**
- **Launch at 15–18% take** to attract laundromat partners and gig washers
- Compete on lower commission vs. Poplin's 25% and Hampr's 30%
- Move to **20–25%** at scale as value-added services (scheduling, insurance, payments, customer acquisition) justify the increase

### Markup Analysis: What Makes Sense

The goal is a pricing structure where the laundromat earns margin, the platform earns commission, and the customer sees transparent, fair value.

#### Example: Hypoallergenic Detergent Upcharge

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Customer charge for upgrade | **$2.50–$5.00/order** (or $0.10–$0.25/lb) |
| Actual incremental cost (premium detergent) | **$0.15–$0.40/load** (bulk Fragrance-Free = ~$0.06–$0.15/lb) |
| Gross margin on upgrade | **80–95%** |
| Platform take (25%) | ~$0.63–$1.25 |
| Laundromat/Washer net | ~$1.88–$3.75 |

**Takeaway:** Customization add-ons are extremely high-margin. The actual cost difference between standard and premium detergent is $0.10–$0.30/load, while the customer pays $2–$5. This is the highest-margin line item in the service.

#### Example: Fabric Softener Add-on

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Customer charge | **$0.50–$2.00/load** |
| Actual cost (bulk liquid softener) | **$0.10–$0.20/load** |
| Gross margin | **75–90%** |

#### Example: Express / Rush Premium

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Customer pays (Poplin express) | **$2.00/lb** vs. $1.00/lb standard |
| Incremental cost to process same-day | **~$0.10–$0.20/lb** (labor reallocation) |
| Extra revenue vs. extra cost | **+$1.00/lb revenue, +$0.15/lb cost** |
| Platform extra take (25% of premium) | ~**+$0.25/lb** |
| Washer extra (75% of premium) | ~**+$0.75/lb** |

**Rush/Express is the single best upsell in the business.** Nearly all the incremental revenue falls to gross profit.

## 7. Platform Economics: How Everyone Wins

### Recommended Customization Pricing Model for a New Platform

The platform should act as a **pass-through with margin on customizations**. The laundromat should not need to absorb add-on costs — the platform should price add-ons to cover: (1) actual incremental material cost + (2) platform commission + (3) meaningful margin to the laundromat partner.

| Customization | Suggested Customer Price | Est. Incremental Cost | Laundromat Net (75%) | Platform Net (25%) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hypoallergenic detergent | **$3.00/order** | $0.20 | $2.25 | $0.75 |
| Scented / brand-name upgrade | **$2.50/order** | $0.15 | $1.88 | $0.63 |
| Fabric softener | **$1.50/order** | $0.15 | $1.13 | $0.38 |
| OxiClean add-on | **$1.50/order** | $0.25 | $1.13 | $0.38 |

**Scenario: Customer orders 20 lbs at $2.00/lb + hypoallergenic detergent ($3.00)**

### Key Strategic Insights for Platform Design

1. **Keep base rate competitive** — Poplin's $1/lb success proves price sensitivity. A new platform attacking mid-market at $1.25–$1.50/lb with free delivery can compete on value.

2. **Build revenue through add-ons and express** — The base rate attracts, but margin comes from: (a) rush/express premium, (b) subscription plans, (c) customization upsells. These are high-margin and low-friction.

3. **Start with lower platform take rate (15–18%) to win supply** — Laundromats and independent washers will defect from Poplin (25%) and Hampr (30%) for better economics. Use this to build supply-side density, then grow take rate as network effects kick in.

4. **Offer subscription early** — Rinse's model shows subscriptions stabilize revenue and increase retention. Even a simple $29/month membership that bundles "free delivery" on all orders dramatically increases LTV.

5. **Customizations must be pre-set, not friction-heavy** — Customers set preferences once and they auto-apply. Conversion on add-ons drops >60% if customers must re-select on every order.

6. **Minimums protect per-order economics** — A $30 minimum on standard and $40 on express ensures no order destroys the unit economics even with driver cost.
